title: Rethinking where to post on the internet
---
Something that has increasingly become clear to me is that internet communication has lost its depth.

In the early days of the internet, if you wanted to put information out there, you created an html document and put it on a web server. Over time that has changed to posting short comments on social media sites and fine-tuning your writing skills to get the most upvotes/shares/interactions (depending on the platform). In contrast with the early days where you would put things on the internet because you thought they were worth sharing with others, we've moved to a regime where others give you feedback in various forms and the system is designed to make you feel as if you're doing it wrong if you don't pursue positive feedback.

This has resulted in a loss of depth. Nobody on social media is going to give you positive feedback because you thought about something for 20 hours before you started writing. The current system rewards fast information consumption, largely for the entertainment value, rather than laying out your full thought process.

Now, I'm not saying others are going to care to read about your full thought process. What matters to you is thinking through the topic and then sharing it with the world. *That doesn't work if you seek feedback from others because there's little entertainment value in someone else's thinking.* The beauty of the early internet was that there was, for the most part, no mechanism for feedback.

When I look back at my participation in various forms of social media, I think about all the hours I spent sharing my thoughts in a format someone else designed. All of those hours were wasted. I cannot think of anything I have gained from posting comments on social media. Not a single thing.

The good news is that we haven't lost what we had in the early days of the internet. I can post something on my website in static html, with no comment section, without sharing it with anyone else. Visitors to my website can read what I've written, but I have no knowledge how many (if any at all) have read it, and I don't know if they thought it was a masterpiece, or stupid, or pointless. I don't care, because I don't have any statistics or comments. I just dump it out into the world because I want to write it and share it with the world, full stop.

This feels more pressing in the age of AI. There needs to be an anchor for the AI models to train on. True, it's not my job to create training data for LLMs, but that's not the reason I would write. It's just a nice side effect for society.

And now I will post this. I will do so having no way to know if even a single other person will ever read it. And that is a relief. My work on this post *ends* with me posting it on my schedule, as opposed to social media, where the feedback process *begins* with posting.

PS: I wasn't going to do this, but I changed my mind. I wrote this post after reading this comment:

> Bonds are more volatile than stocks over 20-year time horizons and underperform stocks during periods of high inflation because of the duration risk. Because of this, I’m more comfortable owning 100% stocks in retirement.

I wanted to fire off a reply to that comment asking how that argument (which is definitely not true as a blanket statement) implies holding 100% stocks in retirement. Are they saying they don't plan to consume from now until 2046?

Old me would have spent several minutes crafting a reply. It wouldn't have been a good use of my time, but it's just so hard to resist when you see something dumb like that. It's not like the commenter is going to say "Oh, I guess my comment didn't make sense. Thanks for opening my eyes to that." I've reached the point that I pass about 90% of the time when I feel compelled to respond. The optimal percentage is 100, so I'm working on it. Other people will say dumb things. Nothing is going to change if I respond, even in deeply illogical cases like this one, so I need to let it go.
