---
I've been making an effort to change my web habits. I had too much of a small number of websites, and was limiting my "writing" (should you want to call it that) to comments. In other words, my use of the web was a complete waste of time.

Change #1 is spending less time on the web. Not much needs to be said about the benefits of that. Related to this topic, I am trying to read more books and papers (the ones printed on paper) and taking notes. The joy of deep learning - something you can not and will not get from social media scrolling.

Change #2 is to stop posting comments on other people's sites. Few people will read them, and even if they do, they're only shallow levels of communication. Nobody benefits from those comments other than links to related material, factual corrections with sources, and so on.

Change #3 is to greatly increase the range of sites I'm visiting. [https://bubbles.town/](https://bubbles.town/) provides access to content that would never take off on social media or one of the big sites. It has been particularly helpful to check the [most recent posts](https://bearblog.dev/discover/?newest=true) on [Bear Blog](https://bearblog.dev/). And I even threw together [a page with my own web directory](https://bachmeil.github.io/the-blog/2026/06/20/finding-content.html).

Change #4 is to increase the writing on this blog, on my website, and on my other blog. I've learned that *writing on the internet is not something you do for other people, it's something you do for your own benefit*. That's not to say it won't be useful to others, but that's not the point. Writing up your thoughts formally, to the point that you're willing to share them with the world, is a sign that you've actually thought things through. "Writing is thinking" is a phrase that's thrown around, and it's true.
