title: The 4% rule can be dangerous
---
The "4% rule" is a useful rule of thumb to ballpark how much you'll need to save for retirement *as long retirement is in the distant future*. It is the answer to the question "What percentage of my retirement savings, at the time of retirement, can I sustainably spend each year of a thirty-year retirement?" It's an interesting exercise, but keep in mind that it assumes you want an income source that imitates Social Security. It's a guaranteed income that you have coming in every month for thirty years. It's adjusted for the CPI.

One reason it's popular is that you can say "I'm going to spend an inflation-adjusted $100,000 per year in retirement. How much do I need?" And then you answer your question by multiplying the $100,000 by 25 to get a "retirement number" of $2,500,000. If you're 35 and have $100,000 in retirement savings, that gives you an idea of what to shoot for to retire by 40 (you're not going to make it, most likely).

It's an interesting place to start your retirement financial planning, but a terrible place to end it. Multplying your annual expenditures by 25 is a terrible way to make any decisions related to retirement. Here are some obvious problems.

- In order to *guarantee* a particular income, you have to set the withdrawal rate low enough that it accommodates the very worst scenarios. You can spend a lot more than 4% most of the time. Even the creator of the 4% rule recommends starting at 5% or 5.5% and cutting back if things are bad in the initial retirement years. The guarantee requires a low withdrawal rate.
- You can cut back on spending in the future if things are bad. Few people retire with only absolutely necessary expenditures for survival. If you're planning to take four vacations a year, you can at little cost cut back to three. Moreover, spending naturally declines over time for the average retiree. The inflation adjustment is not needed every year for 30 years.
- You have no idea what your expenses will be. This makes it hard to 25x a number because you don't know what it is.

Setting that aside, let's say you're going to follow the 4% rule anyway. You *still* do not want to multiply your annual expenses by 25.

- Temporary expenses by definition go away. Your house will be paid off and your kid will graduate college.
- It's common to bridge an income gap in the early years of retirement. If you're 58, you can't get Social Secuirty until 62 at the earliest. That means you need to make up for the absence of Social Security for four years.
- Expenses are lumpy. A new car, a roof replacement, and major home repairs require a lot of cash when they happen, but unless you're spreading these costs out monthly and putting money in a sinking fund, you want to do your planning taking the lumpy nature of these expenditures into account.
- Expenses will fall due to less saving, lower taxes, and less work-related expenses.
- Expenses may rise due to your energy early in retirement, but that's only the early years of retirement, so you don't multiply this by 25.

This is why you should use retirement software like Boldin, Maxifi, or Projection Lab. Here's a sketch of how you should determine "enough". As you can see, it gets complex quickly, so use software made for this type of thing or consult a CFP.

- Start with your current living expenses. If you have no idea what they are, you're not in a position to think about how much you need for retirement.
- Pull out the part of your current living expenses that will remain after retirement and that are regular expenses. Car loans, mortgage payments, retirement savings, support for children, work expenses, one-time medical expenses, home maintenance expenditures, etc. all need to be removed since they're not routine living expenses in retirement. Yes, this step is hard, but there's no way to properly plan without a reasonable estimate of your actual routine expenses in retirement. If you're going to move, you need to approximately know how the cost of living differs where you'll be living. Then subtract Social Security, pension, rental, or whatever income you'll have coming in. Multiply this by 25.
- Taxes will change. You need to calculate your tax bill in retirement, taking into account the sources of your income and your total spending. Multiply this by 25.
- Add up what you need for bridge, temporary, and lumpy expenses. A critical one in this category is health insurance. Large, one-time home repairs and new cars too. Long-term care should go in here. If you'll have heavy travel in the early years of retirement, as opposed to every year of retirement, that belongs in this category. Then subtract the amount you'll have in funds that will be spent down. This is the total non-routine expenses you'll have over your retirement.
- Sum the total of portfolio funds and spenddown funds. That's how much you need.
- Next, you can think about the degree of flexibility. If you're willing to cut spending on travel, you should increase the withdrawals for that part of your spending. If you're budgeting for first class travel but it's really no big deal, you might use a 6% or 7% portfolio withdrawal rate for those funds. The more flexible it is, the higher the withdrawal rate, since it's okay if that spending disappears.
- Finally, you can think about further changes to spending such as shifting away from eating in restaurants.

To me, this is a complex and error-prone process. Doing it in a spreadsheet by yourself is probably okay ten years out. As you get closer to pulling the plug, you want to make sure you're doing it right.
